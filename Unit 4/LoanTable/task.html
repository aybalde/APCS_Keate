LoanTable

Whenever you make a big purchase and choose to take out a loan, there is interest involved. This is how banks make money! We put our money in them to store and they pay us interest - because they are using that capital to loan money to others for these big purchases. The interest rate they give you to store your money is almost always lower than the interest rate they charge you for large loans. They make money on the difference between the amount you pay them to use their money and the amount they make to loan it out for big purchases. Of course, they have some capital to begin with, to protect our assets.

What does this mean for you as the consumer - the one buying your first car or your new home? You will almost always pay more for the car or home than the listed price over time. This is due to your interest payments, and interest matters. The higher the interest you pay, the more your overall cost and payments will be. But how much more?

Often times, interest rates are compounded monthly - this is important because there a specific formula to compound interest rates in a given period. A formula for your monthly payment, which includes part of the principal (the base price of the item) and interest is as follows:
mp = (p*(ar/12)*(1+(ar/12))y * 12)/((1+(ar/12))y * 12-1)
where
mp = monthly payment amount
p = principal, the price of the item
ar = annual interest rate
y = years
